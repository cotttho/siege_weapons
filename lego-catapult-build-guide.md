# LEGO Catapult Build Guide — "The Stone Storm"

A large-scale, high-power torsion catapult (onager-style) built from LEGO Technic and standard bricks. This design uses a single massive throwing arm powered by stacked rubber-band torsion bundles, a reinforced torsion frame, a bucket-style projectile holder, and a heavy double-wide base with outrigger feet. Unlike a trebuchet (which uses a counterweight) or a ballista (which fires bolts), this catapult stores energy in twisted torsion bundles and releases it through a single arm that slams into a crossbar, lobbing projectiles in a high arc.

Scale: ~30 x 20 studs base footprint, ~16 studs tall at the torsion frame. Capable of lobbing small LEGO bricks and balls across a large room.

```
        ║    ║          ║    ║
        ╚════╤══════════╤════╝
             │          │
    ═════════╧══════════╧═══════════
    │          DOUBLE-WIDE BASE         │
    │  ┌────┐                  ┌────┐   │
    │  │FOOT│                  │FOOT│   │
    └──┴────┴──────────────────┴────┴───┘
         ^^outriggers front & rear^^
```

- Single powerful arm driven by rubber-band torsion bundles
- Arm slams into a padded crossbar, transferring energy to the projectile
- Bucket holds multiple projectiles for scatter-shot capability
- Heavy base with outrigger feet absorbs the violent forward kick
- Torsion frame is fully braced to handle repeated high-force impacts

## Build Instructions

### Step 1: Build the Double-Wide Base Frame

The catapult kicks forward violently when fired. The base must be heavy, wide, and anchored.

1. Take four **15-stud Technic beams**. Arrange them in two parallel pairs:
   - Pair A: two beams side by side (touching), forming the left rail
   - Pair B: two beams side by side, forming the right rail
2. Space the two pairs **10 studs apart** (inner beam to inner beam, hole center to hole center).
3. Connect the pairs with **5-stud Technic beams** as cross-braces at five points:
   - Stud 1 (front)
   - Stud 4
   - Stud 8 (center)
   - Stud 12
   - Stud 15 (rear)
4. Pin through both beams of each pair with **Technic friction pins** for maximum rigidity.
5. Add diagonal bracing underneath: attach **7-stud Technic beams** in an X-pattern between the cross-braces at studs 4 and 12. This prevents the base from racking (twisting) under the impact of firing.
6. Flip the base over and attach outrigger feet — these extend well beyond the base on all sides:
   - Front: two **2x10 plates** extending forward past stud 1 by at least 4 studs
   - Rear: two **2x10 plates** extending backward past stud 15 by at least 4 studs
   - Left side: two **2x8 plates** extending outward past the left rail
   - Right side: two **2x8 plates** extending outward past the right rail
7. Add ballast: stack **2x4 bricks** (6–8 total) on the underside of the base near the rear. The catapult kicks forward, so rear weight counteracts this.
8. Flip back upright.

**Result:** A massive, heavy base ~30 x 20 studs (including outriggers) that resists the forward kick of firing.

---

### Step 2: Build the Torsion Frame

This is the heart of the catapult — a tall, rigid frame that holds the torsion axle, the throwing arm, and the crossbar stop. It must withstand repeated violent impacts.

#### Build the four vertical posts:

1. Take four **13-stud Technic beams** — these are the vertical posts.
2. Arrange them in a rectangle: two on the left side, two on the right side, each pair spaced **4 studs apart** (front-to-back), with the left and right pairs spaced **10 studs apart** (matching the base width).

#### Connect the posts into a rigid box frame:

3. Connect the two left posts with horizontal **9-stud Technic beams** at three heights:
   - Hole 2 (bottom)
   - Hole 7 (middle — this is where the torsion axle goes)
   - Hole 12 (top)
4. Repeat for the two right posts.
5. Connect the left frame to the right frame with **7-stud Technic beams** at the same three heights (holes 2, 7, and 12), running across the front and back.
6. You now have a rigid box frame with horizontal bracing on all four sides at three levels.

#### Add diagonal bracing:

7. On the front face of the frame, attach a **9-stud Technic beam** diagonally from hole 2 of the bottom-left to hole 12 of the top-right.
8. On the rear face, attach another diagonal from bottom-right to top-left.
9. These X-braces prevent the frame from folding in any direction.

#### Mount the frame to the base:

10. Position the torsion frame on the base centered at **stud positions 5–9**.
11. Pin the bottom of each vertical post (holes 1–2) into the base rails using **Technic friction pins** and **axle-pin combos** — use both types for maximum grip. Each post should have at least 2 pins connecting it to the base.
12. Add rear buttresses: attach two **9-stud Technic beams** from hole 10 of the rear posts, angling back and down to the base rails at stud 13–14. These absorb the forward kick.

**Result:** A tall (16 studs), rigid box frame bolted to the base with rear buttresses. This frame can take a beating.

---

### Step 3: Install the Torsion Axle and Throwing Arm

The torsion axle runs horizontally through the frame at hole 7. The throwing arm is fixed to this axle and rotates with it.

#### Install the torsion axle:

1. Slide the **12-stud Technic axle** horizontally through **hole 7** of all four vertical posts (left-front, left-rear, right-front, right-rear).
2. Place **half-bushes** on the axle between each post to maintain spacing.
3. Place **full bushes** on the outside of the two outermost posts to prevent the axle from sliding out.

#### Attach the throwing arm:

4. Take the **11-stud Technic beam** — this is the throwing arm.
5. Fix the arm to the torsion axle at **hole 1** of the arm (the bottom end). The arm must be rigidly attached to the axle — it rotates WITH the axle, not on it. Use a **friction pin** through the arm and into the axle, or use a Technic connector that locks the arm to the axle.
6. The arm should point straight up when at rest, with 10 studs of arm above the axle.
7. Verify: when you rotate the axle by hand, the arm should move with it as a single unit. If the arm spins freely on the axle, it won't work — the torsion must drive the arm.

**Result:** The throwing arm is locked to the torsion axle at the center of the frame, pointing upward, ready to be powered by rubber bands.

---

### Step 4: Build the Crossbar (Arm Stop)

When the arm swings forward, it must slam into a padded crossbar at the top of the frame. This sudden stop transfers all the arm's kinetic energy into the projectile, launching it forward in a high arc.

1. Slide the **10-stud Technic axle** horizontally through **hole 12** of the two front vertical posts (left-front and right-front). This is the crossbar.
2. Secure it with **full bushes** on the outside of each post.
3. The crossbar should be rigid and not flex when struck.

#### Reinforce the crossbar:

4. Attach **Technic liftarm connectors (T-shape)** to the crossbar at two points, connecting down to the horizontal brace at hole 7. This transfers the impact force into the frame rather than just the two front posts.
5. On the face of the crossbar where the arm will strike, attach **2x2 plates** as cushion pads (2–3 stacked). This:
   - Spreads the impact force over a larger area
   - Reduces wear on the arm and crossbar
   - Dampens the shock slightly to protect the frame

#### Crossbar angle (advanced tuning):

6. The crossbar position determines the launch angle:
   - At hole 12 (nearly vertical stop): arm stops almost upright → projectile launches at ~70–80° (high arc, short range)
   - Moving the crossbar forward (angling the stop): arm stops past vertical → projectile launches at ~45–60° (optimal range)
7. For maximum range, you can mount the crossbar on a slight forward angle by using offset connectors. Start with hole 12 (vertical stop) and adjust after testing.

**Result:** A reinforced crossbar at the top-front of the frame that stops the arm and determines the launch angle.

---

### Step 5: Attach the Torsion Rubber Bands

The rubber bands wrap around the torsion axle and pull the arm forward. This is the power source.

#### Winding method (most powerful):

1. Pull the throwing arm all the way back (toward the rear of the catapult) and hold it or latch it with the trigger.
2. Take **4–6 thick rubber bands**.
3. Loop each rubber band around the **torsion axle** on the LEFT side of the arm (between the left-front and left-rear posts).
4. Twist each band around the axle 2–3 times to pre-tension it.
5. Repeat with **4–6 thick rubber bands** on the RIGHT side of the arm (between the right-front and right-rear posts).
6. The bands should be pulling the axle (and therefore the arm) forward toward the crossbar.

#### Straight-pull method (easier to set up):

1. Loop **4–6 rubber bands** around the throwing arm near hole 3 (just above the axle).
2. Stretch them forward and loop the other ends around the **front base cross-brace** (stud 1) or the front horizontal brace of the torsion frame (hole 2).
3. Repeat on both sides of the arm for even pull.

#### Power levels:

| Bands total | Power level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 4–6 | Medium | Good for testing and tuning |
| 8–10 | High | Standard operation |
| 12+ | Maximum | Check that crossbar, frame, and trigger can handle the force |

**Result:** The arm is spring-loaded and snaps forward into the crossbar when released.

---

### Step 6: Build the Projectile Bucket

Unlike a ballista (which fires a single bolt), a catapult uses a bucket that can hold one large projectile or multiple small ones for scatter-shot.

#### Build the bucket:

1. Take two **4x6 plates** — stack them for a sturdy bucket floor.
2. Build walls around three sides using **1x4 bricks** and **1x2 bricks**, stacked 2–3 bricks high. Leave the front side open (this is where projectiles fly out).
3. The bucket interior should be roughly 3x5 studs — large enough to hold several 2x2 round bricks or a handful of 1x1 round plates.

#### Attach the bucket to the arm:

4. Attach two **3-stud Technic beams** to the back wall of the bucket, one on each side, pointing downward. These are the hinge arms.
5. Connect the hinge arms to the **top hole (hole 11)** of the throwing arm using a **6-stud Technic axle** running through both hinge arms and the arm tip. Use **frictionless pins** — the bucket must swing freely.
6. The bucket should hang level when the arm is upright, and swing forward (opening facing the target) as the arm rotates.

#### Why a hinged bucket matters:

- When the arm slams into the crossbar, the bucket continues to rotate forward due to inertia
- This whip effect adds velocity to the projectile
- The open front of the bucket faces the target at the moment of release
- Projectiles fly out in a high arc

**Result:** A hinged bucket at the arm tip that whips forward on impact, launching projectiles in a high arc.

---

### Step 7: Build the Trigger Mechanism

The trigger must hold the arm in the cocked (pulled-back) position against the full force of the rubber bands, then release cleanly.

#### Build the trigger latch:

1. At the **rear of the base (stud 13–14)**, build a trigger post: a **3-stud Technic beam** standing vertically, pinned to the base rail.
2. Attach a **5-stud Technic beam** to the top of this post using a **frictionless pin** so it can pivot horizontally. This is the trigger arm.
3. At the end of the trigger arm (pointing toward the center of the base), insert a **2-stud axle** pointing upward — this is the catch pin.

#### Cock the catapult:

4. Pull the throwing arm all the way back until it's nearly horizontal, pointing toward the rear.
5. A **Technic pin** protruding from the side of the throwing arm near hole 9 hooks under the trigger arm's catch pin.
6. The rubber bands are now fully stretched, and the trigger holds everything in place.

#### Fire:

7. Push the trigger arm sideways. The catch pin slides out from under the arm pin, and the arm snaps forward.

#### Trigger return springs:

8. Attach two **medium rubber bands** from the trigger arm to the trigger post. These snap the trigger out of the way on release.

#### Remote firing (optional):

9. Tie a **15 cm cord** to the end of the trigger arm.
10. Run the cord to the side or rear of the catapult.
11. Pull the cord to fire from a safe distance — useful at maximum power when the arm slams forward violently.

**Result:** A mechanical trigger with optional remote pull cord for safe firing.

---

### Step 8: Final Assembly and Pre-Flight Check

1. Ensure the base sits flat on a hard surface. Any wobble wastes energy and can cause the catapult to walk sideways.
2. Cock the catapult: pull the arm back, engage the trigger.
3. Verify the arm clears the torsion frame on both sides as it swings forward.
4. Verify the bucket clears the crossbar and frame as it whips over the top.
5. Load projectiles into the bucket:
   - Single shot: one **2x2 round brick** centered in the bucket
   - Scatter shot: four to six **1x1 round plates** spread in the bucket
6. Clear the firing range — expect launches of **4–7 meters** depending on ammo and power level.
7. Fire using the trigger lever or pull cord.

## Tuning for Maximum Power

| Adjustment | Effect |
|---|---|
| Add more rubber bands | More torsion force, faster arm swing, longer throws |
| Use thicker rubber bands | More energy stored per band |
| Pre-twist the torsion bands more | Higher starting tension, more explosive release |
| Move crossbar forward (past vertical) | Lower launch angle, longer range (optimal ~45°) |
| Add more crossbar padding | Protects the frame, slightly softer stop (less jarring) |
| Use a lighter bucket | Less mass at the arm tip = faster swing |
| Use lighter projectiles | Faster launch, longer arc |
| Clamp the base to a table | Eliminates all recoil — single biggest improvement |
| Add more rear ballast | Counteracts forward kick without clamping |
| Lengthen the arm (13-stud beam) | More tip speed, but needs more torsion to compensate |
| Add a sling instead of a bucket | Adds whip effect like a trebuchet — harder to tune but more range |

## Safety Notes

- This catapult launches projectiles with real force in a high arc. Always aim away from people, pets, and breakable objects.
- The arm slams into the crossbar violently — keep all fingers and body parts clear of the arm's swing path.
- Rubber bands under high tension can snap. Inspect before each use and replace if worn or cracked.
- The bucket whips over the top at high speed — ensure nothing is in its path.
- Scatter shot is unpredictable — clear a wide area downrange, not just a narrow lane.
- Use the remote pull cord when firing at maximum power.
- Test outdoors or in a gymnasium. The high arc means projectiles can hit ceilings indoors.
- Wear eye protection during close-range testing.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Projectile goes straight up | Crossbar is stopping the arm too early (too vertical). Move the crossbar forward or angle it. |
| Projectile goes into the ground | Crossbar is too far forward, arm is past optimal angle. Move crossbar back. |
| Arm doesn't hit crossbar hard | Add more rubber bands, increase pre-twist on torsion bundles |
| Catapult tips forward on firing | Add more rear ballast, extend rear outrigger feet, or clamp the base |
| Catapult walks sideways | Rubber bands are uneven left-to-right. Equalize band count and tension on both sides |
| Bucket spills projectiles before launch | Build bucket walls higher, or add a thin plate as a lid that pops off on impact |
| Arm hits the torsion frame sides | Arm is off-center on the axle. Re-center with half-bushes |
| Crossbar breaks or flexes | Reinforce with T-connectors down to the mid-frame brace, use a thicker axle |
| Frame wobbles after repeated firing | Check all pins are fully seated, add more diagonal bracing, tighten connections |
| Trigger can't hold the arm | Increase catch pin length, add friction to trigger pivot, reinforce trigger post |
| Rubber bands lose power quickly | Replace with fresh bands between sessions, store un-stretched |

## Catapult vs. Trebuchet vs. Ballista

| Feature | Catapult (this build) | Trebuchet | Ballista |
|---|---|---|---|
| Power source | Torsion (rubber bands) | Gravity (counterweight) | Torsion (rubber bands) |
| Projectile path | High arc | High arc | Flat trajectory |
| Best for | Area bombardment, scatter shot | Heavy single projectiles, max range | Precision single bolt, accuracy |
