# LEGO Ballista Build Guide — "The Bolt Thrower"

A powerful, functional ballista built from LEGO Technic and standard bricks. This design uses dual rubber-band torsion bundles and a sliding bolt channel to launch projectiles with serious force.

---

## Design Overview

```
         ===== BOLT (Technic axle) =====>
        ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
        │          BOLT CHANNEL            │
   ╔════╪════╗                        ╔════╪════╗
   ║  TORSION ║                        ║  TORSION ║
   ║  BUNDLE  ║──── ARM ────┐    ┌──── ARM ────║  BUNDLE  ║
   ╚════╤════╝              │    │              ╚════╤════╝
        │              STRING/CORD              │
        │                  │                         │
        └──────────────────┼──────────────────┘
                           │
                    TRIGGER MECH
        ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
        │             BASE FRAME           │
        │  ┌────┐                  ┌────┐  │
        │  │FOOT│                  │FOOT│  │
        └──┴────┴──────────────────┴────┴──┘
```

- Two torsion arms powered by rubber bands provide the launch force
- A central channel guides the bolt (long Technic axle) for accuracy
- A drawstring connects the two arm tips and pushes the bolt forward
- A trigger latch holds the string back until release

## Build Instructions

### Step 1: Build the Base Frame

The base must be rigid and heavy enough to stay planted during firing.

1. Lay the two **15-stud Technic beams** parallel, spaced **6 studs apart** (hole center to hole center).
2. Connect them with three **5-stud Technic beams** as cross-braces:
   - Front: studs 1–2
   - Center: studs 7–8
   - Rear: studs 14–15
3. Secure each cross-brace with **Technic friction pins** into the holes of the parallel beams.
4. Underneath the base, attach two **2x8 plates** running lengthwise along each rail for added weight and rigidity.
5. Attach two **2x6 plates** underneath at the front and rear, extending outward past the rails to act as stabilizer feet.

**Result:** A solid rectangular base, ~15 x 10 studs, that sits flat and won't slide.

---

### Step 2: Build the Torsion Arm Mounts

These are the two vertical frames that hold the throwing arms and the rubber-band torsion bundles. You'll build one on each side of the base.

#### For each side (build two, mirror image):

1. Take a **9-stud Technic beam** — this is the vertical mount post.
2. Stand it upright and attach it to the base rail at **stud position 4** using two **Technic friction pins** through the bottom two holes into the base beam. The mount should be perpendicular to the base, pointing straight up.
3. Reinforce the base of the mount: attach a **3-stud Technic beam** as a diagonal brace from hole 3 of the mount down to hole 6 of the base rail. Secure with **friction pins**. This prevents the mount from flexing backward during firing.
4. At the **top of the mount (hole 9)** and at **hole 5 (middle)**, these will be the anchor points for the torsion axle.

#### Connect the two mounts:

1. Slide an **8-stud Technic axle** horizontally through **hole 7** of the left mount, across the gap, and through **hole 7** of the right mount. This is the torsion arm pivot axle.
2. Place **half-bushes** on the axle on the inside of each mount to keep the axle centered.
3. Place **full bushes** on the outside of each mount to prevent the axle from sliding out.

**Result:** Two vertical posts rising from the base at stud 4, connected by a horizontal axle at hole 7.

---

### Step 3: Build the Throwing Arms

The two arms rotate on the torsion axle and are pulled forward by rubber bands.

#### For each arm (build two):

1. Take a **7-stud Technic beam** — this is one throwing arm.
2. Slide it onto the **8-stud torsion axle** at **hole 1** of the beam (the bottom end). The arm should hang downward and be able to rotate freely on the axle.
3. Position the left arm just inside the left mount, and the right arm just inside the right mount. Use **half-bushes** to keep each arm in position on the axle without binding.
4. At the **top end (hole 7)** of each arm, attach a **Technic angle connector** — this creates a small hook/nub at the tip where you'll tie the drawstring.

#### Add arm stops:

The arms need to stop at the forward position (pointing straight ahead) so they don't over-rotate.

1. On each torsion mount post, attach a **3-stud Technic beam** horizontally at **hole 8** (one hole above the torsion axle), pointing inward toward the bolt channel.
2. Secure with a **friction pin**.
3. When the arms swing forward, they'll hit these stop beams at roughly the vertical position, transferring all energy into the drawstring.

**Result:** Two arms hanging from the torsion axle that can swing forward and stop at vertical.

---

### Step 4: Attach the Torsion Rubber Bands

This is where the power comes from. The rubber bands act like torsion springs, pulling the arms forward with force.

1. For each arm, take **2–3 thick rubber bands**.
2. Loop them around the **throwing arm** near hole 2 (just above the pivot point).
3. Stretch the rubber bands forward and loop the other end around the **base cross-brace at the front** (stud position 1–2).
4. The rubber bands should be taut when the arms are pulled back (cocked position) and should forcefully pull the arms forward toward the front of the ballista.
5. **More rubber bands = more power.** Start with 2 per side and add more if the bolt isn't launching far enough.
6. Make sure both sides have equal tension so the arms swing symmetrically.

**Alternative torsion method:** Instead of stretching forward, wrap rubber bands around the torsion axle itself between the mount and the arm. Twist the axle to wind them up. This is closer to a real ballista's torsion bundle but harder to set up with LEGO.

**Result:** Both arms are spring-loaded and snap forward when released.

---

### Step 5: Build the Bolt Channel

The channel guides the bolt (projectile) straight and keeps it aligned with the drawstring.

1. Take the two **11-stud Technic beams** — these are the channel rails.
2. Lay them on top of the base, parallel to the base rails, spaced **2 studs apart** (just wide enough for a Technic axle to slide between them).
3. Position them centered on the base, running from **stud 4** (at the torsion mounts) all the way to **stud 15** (the rear of the base).
4. Attach them to the base using **Technic perpendicular connectors** at 3–4 points along their length. Pin the connectors into the base rails below and the channel rails above.
5. Line the floor of the channel with **2x4 plates** to create a smooth, flat surface for the bolt to slide on.
6. At the front opening of the channel (stud 4), attach **1x2 plates with rails** on each side — these act as final guides to keep the bolt centered as it exits.

**Result:** A straight, smooth channel running down the center of the ballista from the torsion mounts to the rear.

---

### Step 6: Build the Trigger Mechanism

The trigger holds the drawstring in the cocked (pulled-back) position and releases it on command.

1. At the **rear of the bolt channel (stud 14–15)**, attach a **3-stud Technic beam** vertically using a **frictionless pin** through the base so it can pivot left-right. This is the trigger lever.
2. At the top of the trigger lever, insert a **2-stud axle** pointing inward over the channel — this is the catch pin.
3. The drawstring will have a loop that hooks over this catch pin when the ballista is cocked.
4. To fire: push the trigger lever sideways. The catch pin moves out from under the string loop, releasing the drawstring and launching the bolt.

#### Reinforced trigger (optional):

1. Add a second **3-stud beam** on the opposite side of the channel, connected to the first with a **5-stud beam** across the top, creating a bridge over the channel.
2. The catch pin sits on this bridge. Pushing either side releases it.
3. This is more stable and less likely to accidentally release.

**Result:** A pivoting trigger that holds and releases the drawstring reliably.

---

### Step 7: String the Drawstring

1. Cut a piece of **strong cord to ~20 cm**.
2. Tie one end to the **tip of the left throwing arm** (through the angle connector hole at the top).
3. Run the string across the front of the bolt channel.
4. Tie the other end to the **tip of the right throwing arm**.
5. The string should have a small amount of slack when the arms are in the forward (resting) position.
6. In the **center of the string**, tie a small loop or knot — this is the nock point that pushes the bolt and hooks onto the trigger catch.
7. Pull both arms back (against the rubber band tension) until the center loop reaches the trigger catch pin at the rear of the channel. Hook the loop over the catch pin.

**Result:** The ballista is now cocked — the arms are held back by the trigger, and the string is taut across the channel.

---

### Step 8: Load and Fire

1. Place the **12-stud Technic axle** (the bolt) into the channel from the rear, sliding it forward until the back end rests against the drawstring's center knot.
2. Optionally, press a **1x1 round plate** onto the front tip of the axle for a blunt nose.
3. Aim the ballista at your target.
4. Push the **trigger lever** sideways.
5. The drawstring releases, the arms snap forward, and the bolt launches down the channel.

## Tuning for Maximum Power

| Adjustment | Effect |
|---|---|
| Add more rubber bands | More force on the arms, faster snap, longer shots |
| Use thicker rubber bands | More energy stored per band |
| Longer arms | More drawstring travel, higher bolt speed (but need stronger rubber bands) |
| Lighter bolt | Faster launch, longer range |
| Smoother channel floor | Less friction, bolt exits faster |
| Equal tension both sides | Straight shots — uneven tension causes the bolt to veer |
| Tighter drawstring | Less energy lost to string slack |
| Move arm stops slightly past vertical | Arms transfer energy over a longer arc |

| Multi-shot | Three 6-stud axles side by side | Spread shot, shorter range |

## Safety Notes

- Always aim away from people, pets, and anything breakable.
- Technic axles are pointy — never aim at faces. Use blunt tips (round plates) on bolts.
- Rubber bands can snap — inspect them before each use and replace if worn.
- Keep fingers clear of the arms and drawstring when the ballista is cocked.
- Test in a large open space.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Bolt doesn't launch far | Add more rubber bands, check for channel friction, ensure arms hit the stops |
| Bolt veers left or right | Equalize rubber band tension on both sides, center the drawstring knot |
| Arms don't stop cleanly | Adjust arm stop beams, add padding (small plate) to the stop surface |
| Trigger releases accidentally | Add more friction to the trigger pivot, make the catch pin longer |
| Drawstring slips off arm tips | Tie more secure knots, use a groove in the angle connector |
| Rubber bands keep snapping | Use fresh bands, reduce the pull-back distance slightly |
| Base slides on firing | Add weight to the base, use rubber feet underneath, or tape down |
| Arms swing unevenly | Check that both arms are mounted at the same position on the axle |

## Historical Note

The ballista was one of the most feared siege weapons of the ancient world, used by the Greeks and Romans. Unlike a catapult that lobs projectiles in an arc, a ballista fires bolts on a flat trajectory with tremendous force and accuracy — essentially a giant crossbow powered by torsion bundles. This LEGO version captures that same principle: stored torsion energy released through a guided channel for accurate, powerful shots.
