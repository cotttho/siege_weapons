# LEGO Ballista Build Guide — "The Siege Striker"

A large-scale, high-power ballista built from LEGO Technic and standard bricks. This design features a reinforced double-wide base, tall torsion frames, long compound throwing arms, a full-length guided bolt channel, and a heavy-duty trigger mechanism. Powered by stacked rubber-band torsion bundles for maximum bolt velocity.

Scale: ~30 x 16 studs base footprint, ~14 studs tall at the torsion mounts. Capable of launching Technic axle bolts across a large room.

## Build Instructions

### Step 1: Build the Double-Wide Base Frame

A larger ballista generates significant recoil. The base must be heavy and rigid.

1. Take four **15-stud Technic beams**. Arrange them in two parallel pairs:
   - Pair A: two beams side by side (touching), forming the left rail
   - Pair B: two beams side by side, forming the right rail
2. Space the two pairs **8 studs apart** (inner beam to inner beam, hole center to hole center).
3. Connect the pairs with **5-stud Technic beams** as cross-braces at five points:
   - Stud 1 (front)
   - Stud 4
   - Stud 8 (center)
   - Stud 12
   - Stud 15 (rear)
4. Pin through both beams of each pair with **Technic friction pins** for maximum rigidity.
5. Add diagonal bracing: attach **7-stud Technic beams** in an X-pattern between the cross-braces at studs 4 and 12, underneath the base. This prevents the base from twisting under recoil.
6. Flip the base over and attach stabilizer feet:
   - Four **2x10 plates** along the front and rear edges, extending outward
   - Four **2x8 plates** along the left and right sides, extending outward
7. For extra ballast, stack **2x4 bricks** (8 total) on the underside of the base near the center. This adds dead weight to resist recoil.
8. Flip back upright.

**Result:** A massive, heavy rectangular frame ~30 x 16 studs with wide feet and ballast. This base won't budge.

---

### Step 2: Build the Torsion Arm Frames

These are tall, reinforced vertical frames that hold the throwing arms and absorb the shock of the arms slamming into the stops. Build one on each side.

#### For each side (build two, mirror image):

1. Take two **9-stud Technic beams** — these are the vertical posts of the torsion frame.
2. Stand them upright, **4 studs apart**, and connect them at three heights with horizontal beams:
   - **5-stud beam** at hole 2 (bottom brace)
   - **5-stud beam** at hole 5 (middle brace)
   - **5-stud beam** at hole 8 (top brace)
3. This creates a rigid rectangular frame, like a ladder.
4. Add diagonal reinforcement: attach a **7-stud Technic beam** diagonally from hole 2 of the left post to hole 7 of the right post. This triangulates the frame and prevents it from folding under impact.

#### Add rear buttresses:

5. Attach two **7-stud Technic beams** as rear buttresses:
   - One from hole 7 of each vertical post, angling back and down to the base rail at stud 8
6. Secure with **friction pins** and **axle-pin combos**.

#### Mount to the base:

7. Position the left torsion frame on the base at **stud position 4**, with the two vertical posts straddling the left base rail pair.
8. Pin the bottom two holes of each post into the double base beams using **friction pins** through both beams.
9. Repeat for the right torsion frame on the right base rail pair at the same stud position.

#### Connect the two frames:

10. Slide a **12-stud Technic axle** horizontally through **hole 6** of the inner post of the left frame, across the gap, and through **hole 6** of the inner post of the right frame. This is the torsion arm pivot axle.
11. Place **half-bushes** on the axle inside each frame to center it.
12. Place **full bushes** on the outside of each frame to prevent the axle from sliding out.

**Result:** Two tall, rigid torsion frames rising ~14 studs above the base at stud 4, connected by the pivot axle at hole 6.

---

### Step 3: Build the Long Throwing Arms

Longer arms mean more drawstring travel and higher bolt velocity.

#### For each arm (build two):

1. Take an **11-stud Technic beam** — this is one throwing arm.
2. Slide it onto the **12-stud torsion pivot axle** at **hole 1** of the beam (the bottom/pivot end). The arm should hang downward and rotate freely.
3. Position the left arm just inside the left torsion frame, the right arm just inside the right frame.
4. Use **half-bushes** on the axle on both sides of each arm to keep them positioned without binding.
5. At the **top end (hole 11)** of each arm, attach a **Technic angle connector (180°)** — this creates a hook point for the drawstring.

#### Build reinforced arm stops:

The arms slam forward with significant force. The stops must be strong.

6. On each torsion frame, attach a **7-stud Technic beam** horizontally at **hole 7** (one hole above the pivot axle), pointing inward toward the bolt channel. This is the primary stop.
7. Behind the primary stop, attach a **3-stud Technic beam** at **hole 8** as a backup stop.
8. On the face of each stop beam where the arm will impact, attach a **2x2 plate** as a cushion pad. This spreads the impact force and reduces wear.

**Result:** Two long (11-stud) arms on the pivot axle that swing forward and slam into reinforced stops.

---

### Step 4: Attach the Torsion Rubber Bands

More rubber bands = more force. At this scale, you want 4–6 thick bands per arm.

#### For each arm:

1. Take **4 to 6 thick rubber bands** (size 64 or larger).
2. Loop them around the **throwing arm** near hole 2 (just above the pivot).
3. Stretch the rubber bands forward and loop the other ends around the **front base cross-brace** (stud position 1).
4. The bands should be taut when the arms are in the resting (forward) position and very taut when pulled back to the cocked position.

#### Balancing tension:

5. Count the bands on each side — they must be equal.
6. The bands should be the same length and thickness on both sides.
7. Test by pulling both arms back simultaneously — they should require equal effort and snap forward at the same speed.

#### Power progression:

| Bands per side | Power level | Recommended for |
|---|---|---|
| 2–3 | Medium | Initial testing, tuning |
| 4–5 | High | Standard operation |
| 6+ | Maximum | Maximum range (check that stops and trigger can handle the force) |

**Result:** Both arms are heavily spring-loaded and snap forward with serious force.

---

### Step 5: Build the Full-Length Bolt Channel

A longer channel means the bolt accelerates over a greater distance, resulting in higher exit velocity.

1. Take the four **13-stud Technic beams** — pair them into two double-wide channel rails (two beams per rail, pinned together).
2. Lay the two double-wide rails on top of the base, parallel, spaced **2 studs apart** (just wide enough for a 16-stud Technic axle to slide freely between them).
3. Position them centered on the base, running from **stud 4** (at the torsion frames) all the way to **stud 15** (the rear of the base). If the channel needs to extend past the base, support the rear overhang with additional beams.
4. Attach the channel rails to the base using **Technic perpendicular connectors** at 4–5 points along their length.
5. Line the floor of the channel with **2x6 plates** and **2x4 plates** end-to-end, creating a smooth, continuous surface. Any gaps or bumps will slow the bolt.
6. Build low channel walls: attach **1x6 bricks** and **1x4 bricks** along the outside of each rail, one brick high. These prevent the bolt from jumping out of the channel during launch.
7. At the front opening (stud 4) and rear opening (stud 15), attach **1x2 plates with rails** on each side as precision guides.

**Result:** A long, smooth, walled channel running the full length of the base. The bolt has maximum acceleration distance.

---

### Step 6: Build the Reinforced Trigger Bridge

At this power level, a simple pivoting latch won't hold. You need a bridge-style trigger that spans the channel.

#### Build the trigger bridge:

1. At the **rear of the bolt channel (stud 14–15)**, build two short vertical posts, one on each side of the channel:
   - Each post: a **3-stud Technic beam** standing upright, pinned to the base rail.
2. Connect the tops of the two posts with a **7-stud Technic beam** spanning across the channel. This is the trigger bridge.
3. The bridge should be high enough that the bolt can pass underneath it freely.

#### Build the catch mechanism:

4. On the trigger bridge, mount a **3-stud Technic beam** using a **frictionless pin** so it can pivot. This is the trigger lever.
5. At one end of the trigger lever, insert a **2-stud axle** pointing downward into the channel — this is the catch pin.
6. At the other end of the trigger lever (outside the channel), this is the handle you push to fire.

#### Add trigger return springs:

7. Attach two **medium rubber bands** from the trigger lever to the bridge posts. These snap the catch pin out of the way quickly on release, preventing it from snagging the drawstring.

#### Reinforce the bridge:

8. Add a second **7-stud beam** parallel to the first, one stud behind it, connected to the same posts. This double-bridge resists the forward pull of the drawstring without flexing.

**Result:** A strong trigger bridge that holds the drawstring under high tension and releases cleanly.

---

### Step 7: String the Heavy Drawstring

At this scale, use braided cord rather than thin string — it needs to handle the force of 8–12 rubber bands pulling the arms.

1. Cut a piece of **strong braided cord to ~30 cm**.
2. Tie one end to the **tip of the left throwing arm** (through the angle connector at hole 11). Use a double knot and optionally a drop of glue for security.
3. Run the cord across the front of the bolt channel.
4. Tie the other end to the **tip of the right throwing arm** with the same secure knot.
5. The cord should have slight slack when the arms are in the forward (resting) position.
6. At the **center of the cord**, tie a tight loop knot — this is the nock point. It must be:
   - Large enough to hook over the trigger catch pin
   - Small enough not to snag on the channel walls
   - Positioned exactly at the center so both arms pull evenly
7. Pull both arms back (against all the rubber band tension) until the center loop reaches the trigger catch pin. Hook the loop over the catch pin.

**Result:** The ballista is cocked — arms held back, drawstring taut, ready to fire.

---

### Step 8: Load and Fire

1. Place a **16-stud Technic axle** (the bolt) into the channel from the rear, sliding it forward until the back end rests against the drawstring's center knot.
2. Press a **1x1 round plate** onto the front tip of the axle as a blunt nose cap.
3. Ensure the bolt slides freely in the channel — push it back and forth to check for snags.
4. Aim the ballista at your target.
5. Push the **trigger lever handle** sideways.
6. The catch pin releases the drawstring loop, the arms snap forward into the stops, and the bolt launches down the channel.

## Tuning for Maximum Power

| Adjustment | Effect |
|---|---|
| Add more rubber bands (up to 6 per side) | More arm force, faster snap, longer shots |
| Use thicker/stronger rubber bands | More energy stored per band |
| Use longer arms (swap 11-stud for 13-stud) | More drawstring travel, higher bolt speed (needs stronger bands and stops) |
| Use a lighter bolt (12-stud axle instead of 16) | Faster launch, longer range |
| Polish the channel floor with smooth tiles | Less friction, bolt exits faster |
| Equalize tension on both sides exactly | Straight shots — even small imbalance causes veering |
| Use braided fishing line as drawstring | Stronger, less stretch, more energy transfer |
| Move arm stops slightly past vertical | Arms transfer energy over a longer arc before stopping |
| Clamp the base to a table | Eliminates all recoil energy loss — single biggest improvement |
| Add weight to the base (tape coins underneath) | Reduces recoil without clamping |

## Safety Notes

- This ballista launches bolts with real force and speed. Always aim away from people, pets, and breakable objects.
- Technic axles have pointed ends — always use blunt tip caps (round plates). Never aim at faces.
- Rubber bands are under high tension when cocked — they can snap. Inspect before each use and replace if worn, cracked, or stretched out.
- Keep fingers clear of the arms, drawstring, and channel when the ballista is cocked. The arms slam forward hard enough to pinch or bruise.
- The trigger can release accidentally if bumped — treat a cocked ballista like a loaded weapon.
- Test outdoors or in a large open room. Clear the firing lane completely.
- Wear eye protection during close-range testing.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Bolt doesn't launch far | Add more rubber bands, check for channel friction, ensure arms hit the stops cleanly |
| Bolt veers left or right | Equalize rubber band count and tension on both sides, center the drawstring knot exactly |
| Arms don't stop cleanly / bounce back | Reinforce the arm stops, add cushion pads, check that stops are at the right height |
| Trigger releases accidentally | Increase friction on the trigger pivot, make the catch pin longer, add a safety latch |
| Trigger can't hold the tension | Reinforce the bridge with a second beam, use a thicker catch pin, check that the drawstring loop isn't slipping |
| Drawstring slips off arm tips | Use more secure knots (bowline), add a drop of glue, wrap cord around the connector twice |
| Rubber bands keep snapping | Use fresh bands, reduce the pull-back distance, use thicker bands rated for higher tension |
| Base slides on firing | Add more ballast weight, use rubber feet underneath, or clamp to the table |
| Arms swing unevenly | Verify both arms are at the same position on the axle, check that rubber bands are identical on both sides |
| Bolt jams in the channel | Widen the channel by 1 stud, smooth the floor, check for protruding pins or connectors |
| Channel walls break off on firing | Reinforce wall attachment points, use longer bricks, add plates on top to lock them |

## Historical Note

The ballista was the precision artillery of the ancient world. Roman legions deployed them in batteries, with each ballista capable of punching bolts through shields and armor at ranges exceeding 300 meters. The key to their power was torsion — bundles of sinew or hair twisted to store enormous energy, released through long arms and a guided channel for devastating accuracy. This scaled-up LEGO version captures that same engineering: maximum stored energy, efficient transfer through long arms, and a precision channel for accurate, powerful shots.
